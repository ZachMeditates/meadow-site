Use Case

Personal CRM for networking

You meet 50 people at a conference and forget 45 of them within a week. Here's how to fix that.

The conference problem

You go to a conference. Over two days you meet 30 interesting people. You swap business cards, connect on LinkedIn, maybe jot a few notes in your phone. You feel energized. You tell yourself you'll follow up with all of them.

A week later you can barely remember who said what. The guy from the fintech startup, was his name Jason or Justin? The woman who works on developer tools, which company was that again? You know you had a great conversation about distributed systems with someone at the happy hour, but the details are gone.

The information had a 48 hour shelf life and you let it expire. Those 30 connections become 30 names in a LinkedIn list that you never look at again.

Why most networking tools miss the point

Business CRMs want you to track deals and pipeline stages. That's great if you're a sales rep. It's useless if you just met someone cool at a panel and want to remember what you talked about.

LinkedIn connections are names in a list. You can scroll through them, sure, but there's no context. Nothing that tells you Alex from Stripe mentioned they're moving to Austin, their co-founder just had twins, and they're looking for product people who understand APIs.

Spreadsheets work for about a week. You make a Google Sheet, add a few rows, feel organized. Then the friction of opening it, finding the right row, and typing notes into tiny cells kills your momentum. By conference number two, the spreadsheet is forgotten.

What you actually need is a way to capture details as fast as you can think them, and a system that reminds you to act on those details before the window closes.

How Meadow turns conversations into connections

Meadow lives in Telegram. During or right after a conversation at an event, you text it a quick note. No forms, no apps to open, no fields to fill out. Just a message.

Meadow creates the contact, parses the details, and stores everything. Name, company, what you talked about, what they mentioned about their life. All from a single text.

The following Monday, your weekly brief includes every person you met at the event. For each one, Meadow drafts a follow up message based on what you actually discussed. Not a generic "great meeting you" template. Something specific. Something that shows you were paying attention.

All you have to do is copy, paste, and send.

What networking with Meadow looks like

Say you're at a two day product conference. Here's how the week plays out.

Day one, during the event

Between sessions, you pull out your phone and text Meadow:

"Met Alex Chen from Stripe at the AI panel. They run developer experience. Moving to Austin in the fall. Interested in our API docs approach."

Thirty seconds. Done. Back to the conference.

After lunch, another message:

"Talked to Priya Sharma at the Vercel booth. She's a founding engineer, been there 3 years. We bonded over the pain of docs driven development. She has a talk at React Summit in June."

Day two, on the way home

You fire off a few more while the details are fresh:

"Had drinks with Marcus from Figma and Jamie who freelances in design systems. Marcus is thinking about leaving. Jamie mentioned she's looking for contract work."

Meadow logs both contacts from that single message, tagging the relevant details to each person.

Monday morning

Your weekly brief arrives. It includes a section for your new conference contacts with draft messages:

For Alex Chen: "Hey Alex, good talking at the AI panel last week. Let me know how the Austin move goes. Would love to pick your brain about the developer experience side of things when you're settled."

For Priya Sharma: "Hey Priya, enjoyed our conversation at the Vercel booth. Good luck with the React Summit talk in June. If you want a test audience for a dry run, I'm around."

Each message references something real. Something that makes the other person think: this person actually listened to me.

After the conference

The real value of networking is never the event itself. It's what happens in the months that follow. The problem is that new connections are fragile. If you don't follow up within a week or two, the window closes. After a month, reaching out feels awkward. After three months, you're strangers again.

Meadow tracks every relationship you add. When a new contact starts going cold, it notices. Your weekly brief will flag them: "You met Alex Chen 6 weeks ago and haven't talked since." It nudges you before the connection fades to nothing.

This is the difference between collecting contacts and building a network. Collecting is easy. Building means actually staying in touch, and that's the part nobody does because life gets busy and people fall through the cracks.

Meadow makes sure the cracks are smaller.

FAQ

How do I use Meadow at conferences?

During or after conversations, text Meadow something like: "Met Alex from Stripe at the AI panel, they're hiring for product roles, seemed interested in our API." Meadow creates the contact and stores the context. No app to open, no form to fill out.

Can Meadow help me follow up after events?

Yes. Your Monday brief shows recent contacts and drafts follow up messages based on what you talked about. No more "great meeting you" with zero context. Each draft references the actual conversation you had.

Is Meadow better than a business CRM for networking?

If you're tracking sales leads, use a business CRM. If you're building genuine relationships with people you meet, Meadow's conversational approach makes it much easier to actually capture the details that matter. Business CRMs are designed around deals. Meadow is designed around people.
